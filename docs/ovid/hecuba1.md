[Troia[^1] simul Priamusque cadunt. Priameia[^2] coniunx

perdidit infelix hominis post[^3] omnia formam              **405**

externasque novo latratu[^4] terruit auras,

longus in[^5] angustum qua[^6] clauditur Hellespontus[^7].]

Ilion[^8] ardebat, neque adhuc consederat ignis,

exiguumque senis Priami Iovis ara cruorem

conbiberat, tractata comis antistita[^9] Phoebi              **410**

non profecturas[^10] tendebat ad aethera palmas;

Dardanidas[^11] matres patriorum signa[^12] deorum,

dum licet, amplexas succensaque templa[^13] tenentes

invidiosa trahunt victores praemia[^14] Grai[^15];

mittitur Astyanax[^16] illis de turribus, unde              **415**

pugnantem pro se proavitaque regna tuentem

saepe videre patrem monstratum a matre solebat.

iamque viam suadet Boreas[^17], flatuque secundo[^18]

carbasa[^19] mota sonant: iubet uti[^20] navita ventis;

'Troia, vale[^21]! rapimur' clamant, dant oscula terrae              **420**

Troades[^22] et patriae fumantia tecta relinquunt.

ultima conscendit classem —miserabile visu[^23]! —

in mediis Hecabe[^24] natorum inventa sepulcris:

prensantem[^25] tumulos atque ossibus oscula dantem

Dulichiae[^26] traxere manus, tamen unius[^27] hausit              **425**

inque sinu cineres secum tulit Hectoris haustos[^28];

Hectoris in tumulo canum[^29] de vertice[^30] crinem,

inferias[^31] inopes, crinem[^32] lacrimasque reliquit,

-----



[^1]: *Troia...Hellespontus* : [square brackets] mean that the modern editor of this text feels these lines are inauthentic or supurious, meaning that prehaps Ovid did not write them and they should be taken out. Note, he is not confident enough to actually take them out, but leaves them in with this marking. <pointy brackets> indicate that a word or phrase should be added into a text

[^2]: *Priameia coniunx* : an adjective form of Priam's name. *Priam's spouse* is a good way to render it in English

[^3]: *post omnia* : if you take *perdidit* as "she lost," you can add it in back here, *perdidit post omnia*, "after she lost everything(else)"

[^4]: *novo latratu* : this foreshadows Hecuba's transformation at the end of this episode. *Novus, -a, -um* can also be "unfamiliar, strange," since new things are often strange

[^5]: *in angustum* : *into a narrow (place)*, i.e a strait

[^6]: *qua* : *where*, **ablative of location**

[^7]: *Hellespontus* : The Hellespont, or modern day [Dardanelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardanelles), is a narrow strait of sea before where modern day Istanbul is. It is named after [Helle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helle_(mythology)), a mythological woman who died there. It is located near Troy, and a classic case of Ovid flexing in his reader by name dropping specific places

[^8]: *Ilion* : another name for Troy, hence the *Iliad*

[^9]: *antistita Phoebi* : this high priestess of Apollo is [Cassandra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassandra), who is dragged from the temple of Athena and assaulted by [Ajax the Lesser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajax_the_Lesser). See [*Aeneid* 2.402 - 2.437](https://www.poetryintranslation.com/PITBR/Latin/VirgilAeneidII.php#anchor_Toc536009317)

[^10]: *non profecturas* : *not going to achieve (it)*, stressing that her prayers for safety went unanswered 

[^11]: *Dardanidas* : Trojan

[^12]: *patriorum signa deorum* : take this as the direct object of *amplexas*

[^13]: *templa* : take this as the direct object of *tenentes*

[^14]: *invidiosa praemia* : this is in **apposition** to the *Dardanidas matres*. *The Greek victors dragg the Trojan mothers off **as enviable prizes***

[^15]: *Grai* : the Greeks, an alternate spelling of *Graeci*

[^16]: *Astyanax* : [Astyanax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astyanax) was the son of Hector and Andromache, who is throw of the towers of Troy and killed. Interestingly, it is often Odysseus who does the throwing, but Ovid uses a passive verb here, either to be vague or to not assign blame. Ovid's audience would have known this story well

[^17]: *Boreas* : the north wind

[^18]: *secundo* : *secundus, -a, -um* can also be "favorable," like a "second wind"

[^19]: *carbasa* : sails, which are made of linen

[^20]: *uti* : from the verb *utor, uti, usus sum*. Add some kind of "them" in here as to who is being ordered

[^21]: *vale* : "farewell, goodbye!"

[^22]: *Troades* : Trojan women. There is an [Ancient Greek play](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Trojan_Women) with this same title that Ovid's audience would likely have thought of, since it covers a similar series of events during the Fall of Troy

[^23]: *miserabile visu* : **supine!**

[^24]: *Hecabe* : Hecuba's name is in a Greek declension, so this is her nominative. 

[^25]: *prensantem, dantem* : since you have no named direct object, but see that these participles are accusative, you can figure out that they describe Hecuba. Translate it as "her grasping....her giving...."

[^26]: *Dulichiae manus* : *Dulichius* is an epithet that refers to Odysseus, named after an island near Ithaca. He is the warrior who is alloted Hecuba when the Trojan women are divided up as spoils

[^27]: *unius* : connect this to *Hectoris* later on. *of Hector alone* or *only of Hector*

[^28]: *haustos* : same thing as note X above. We need a direct object of *tulit*, and what else could *haustos* refer to except the previously mentioned *cineres*? So try "she brought them *having been drained* with her."

[^29]: *canum* : *white*, from *canus, -a, -um*

[^30]: *de vertice* : *from the top of her head*

[^31]: *inferias inopes* : *helpless/powerless offerings for the dead*, in **apposition** to the *crimen lacrimasque*

[^32]: *crinem* : the **anaphora** here, and how meager these offerings are, works to increase the pity that the audience feels for Hecuba
